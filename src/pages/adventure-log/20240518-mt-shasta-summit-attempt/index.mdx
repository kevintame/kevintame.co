## May 17 — Drive Day

Left early, drove to Shasta to pick up the climbing permit by 4 PM. Grabbed dinner in town and tried to sleep before the alpine start. The kind of sleep where you're aware of every hour passing.

<Image src={photo2} alt="Shasta from the road on the drive in" className="rounded-2xl" />

## May 18 — Summit Day

Alarm somewhere around midnight. A 1:23 AM start from Bunny Flat at 6,934 feet. The plan: climb Shasta via Avalanche Gulch and ski the whole thing back down.

<Image src={photo3} alt="Headlamp-lit start in the dark at Bunny Flat" className="rounded-2xl" />

The first few hours were headlamp-lit bootpacking in the dark, Petzl beams bouncing off the snow. Baofeng radios clipped to chest straps. No sound except boots kicking into crust and wind somewhere above.

<Image src={photo4} alt="Early morning bootpacking on Avalanche Gulch" className="rounded-2xl" />

By sunrise the scale of the mountain started to reveal itself — the distinctive red and orange volcanic rock towers standing out against white snowfields and deep blue sky.

<Image src={photo5} alt="Sunrise on Avalanche Gulch, volcanic rock towers in the distance" className="rounded-2xl" />


The climb kept going through the morning, grinding upward with skis strapped to packs and crampons biting into firm snow. High winds hammered the upper mountain all day, slowing progress and burning through energy faster than expected. Water ran out.

<Image src={photo7} alt="Mid-mountain on Avalanche Gulch with skis on pack" className="rounded-2xl" />

<Image src={photo8} alt="Looking down from high on the mountain" className="rounded-2xl" />

At 13,322 feet — roughly 857 feet below the summit — Kevin and Graham made the call to turn around. Out of water, sustained high winds, still a long way to go. The right call, even if it stung.

<Image src={photo9} alt="High point at 13,322 feet, summit visible above" className="rounded-2xl" />

<Image src={photo10} alt="The upper mountain and the turn-around point" className="rounded-2xl" />

Then came the payoff.

Transition to ski mode. 52 minutes later they were back at the car, 6,390 vertical feet below. The entire Cascade Range stretched out in every direction during the descent — Adams, Hood, Rainier somewhere in the distance.

## The Takeaway

Sometimes the summit is not the point. 12+ hours on one of the biggest volcanoes in the Lower 48, nearly 6,400 feet of climbing, and a ski line most people only dream about. The mountain said not today at 13,322 feet, but the views from up there — with the entire Cascade Range laid out below — tell the story of a day fully sent.

Next time: more water.
